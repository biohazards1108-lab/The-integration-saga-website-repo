Bloater

← Back to Infected List

Overview

Bloaters are one of the most advanced and horrifying stages of the Z‑Virus infection. After years of fungal growth, the host’s body becomes encased in thick, armor‑like layers of hardened Cordyceps. Their bodies swell to massive proportions, with fungal plates forming natural shields across their torso, limbs, and head. Bloaters are slow, but their durability and raw strength make them catastrophic threats.

Behavior

Bloaters rarely chase prey. Instead, they advance steadily, absorbing damage while unleashing devastating ranged attacks. When cornered or enraged, they can perform powerful melee strikes capable of crushing bone. Their presence often signals a long‑infected zone — areas where the fungus has thrived for years without interruption.

Physical Characteristics

  • Massive fungal armor plating covering most of the body
  • Bulky, swollen physique caused by years of fungal growth
  • Deep, guttural breathing and low vocalizations
  • Limited mobility but overwhelming physical strength
  • Fungal sacs across the torso used for ranged attacks

Abilities

  • Spore Bombs: Bloaters can rip fungal sacs from their bodies and throw them, releasing clouds of toxic spores.
  • Armor Plating: Their fungal armor can deflect bullets and resist fire.
  • Crushing Strength: A single strike can incapacitate or kill instantly.
  • Spore Cloud: When severely damaged, Bloaters may release spores as a defensive reaction.

Weaknesses

  • Extremely slow movement
  • Vulnerable to sustained fire or explosives
  • Weak points beneath armor plates
  • Spore sacs can be detonated with precise shots

Infection Stage

Bloaters represent one of the final stages of the Z‑Virus lifecycle. Hosts who survive for years after infection undergo extreme fungal expansion, with Cordyceps replacing muscle tissue, bone structure, and even parts of the nervous system. Their bodies become living fortresses — slow, but nearly unstoppable.

Lore Notes

PROJECT EDEN researchers theorize that Bloaters may be the closest the Z‑Virus has come to creating a fully autonomous fungal organism. Some logs suggest Bloaters exhibit territorial behavior, guarding areas rich in spores or fungal growth. Their presence may indicate locations of significant infection origin or long‑term fungal evolution.
